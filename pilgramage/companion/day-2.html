Today's Virtue — Mercy

Mercy
Latin misericordia — a heart (cor) that goes out to the wretched (miser)

Yesterday was calling; today is conversion — the slow turning of a life once it has heard. And conversion, in the Gospel, does not begin with our climbing toward God. It begins with mercy reaching down to us where we sit.

Morning Reflection

The road leaves San Miniato the way it found it — on a height — and lets you down into the valley of the Elsa, where the plain gives way to vineyards and the white gravel roads climb between them toward a skyline of towers. It is beautiful country, and it is also a country of thresholds: you are crossing, today, from one kind of land to another. That is the day's inward work as well.

Matthew is not on a mountaintop when Christ finds him. He is at the customs post — the tax booth — doing the one job that made a man both rich and hated, collaborating with Rome against his own people. He is exactly the sort the devout crossed the street to avoid. And it is to him, mid-transaction, that the word comes: Follow me. Conversion here is not the reward of a clean life; it is offered to the compromised, the entangled, the man everyone had written off. “I did not come to call the righteous,” Jesus tells the scandalised, “but sinners.”

Notice, too, that Matthew's response is not a retreat but a feast. He gets up, follows — and then throws open his house to a table full of tax collectors and sinners, with Jesus in the middle of them. Conversion overflows. The mercy that reached him does not make him fastidious; it makes him hospitable to exactly the people he used to be. To turn toward God is, at the same moment, to stop despising the wretched — because you have finally understood that you were one of them, and were loved anyway.

Augustine, who spent years fleeing what was nearer to him than he was to himself, put the ache of it into six words: too late did I love You. The lateness is the whole confession. Conversion is not the discovery of a stranger; it is the recognition, at last, of a mercy that had been sitting beside you at the booth the entire time.

What in me have I long defended as righteous — that Christ came instead to call a sinner, and to heal?

Along the Road

10 min

Today's route, San Miniato to San Gimignano. Map © CyclOSM / OpenStreetMap. Full elevation profile & all stages →

This is classic Tuscany at last: the Val d'Elsa opening in long waves of vineyard and olive, the white unmade roads — strade bianche — ribboning over the crests. These are the hills of the Vernaccia, the ancient white grape grown on San Gimignano's sandstone slopes.7 The riding is steadier and slower than yesterday; the climbing is honest but never cruel.

The road holds to the pilgrim line the whole way. Near the twenty-fourth kilometre, at Gambassi Terme, it passes almost against the wall of a small Romanesque church — a natural place to stop.

Pieve di Santa Maria a Chianni, Gambassi Terme

What
A Romanesque pieve (rural parish church) beside Gambassi Terme, standing directly on the Via Francigena — the day's track passes within some 170 metres of it.6
Architecture
Romanesque, of the twelfth century — the plain, strong stone of the pilgrim churches of the Elsa valley.6
Why it mattered
A wayside church on the great road: shade, water, and a threshold to cross for the pilgrim between San Miniato and San Gimignano. Gambassi was itself a Francigena station.
To observe
The Romanesque apse and the bare interior — architecture that asks for silence rather than admiration.
A prayer
At the door, the day's line: “I desire mercy, not sacrifice.”
Practical

Then, for the last hour, the towers. San Gimignano stands on its hill behind a skyline that has almost no equal in Italy. At the height of the Middle Ages the town raised some seventy-two tower-houses, up to seventy metres tall, each family answering another's height with its own; fourteen still stand, where nearly everywhere else such towers were pulled down.7 They are monuments, at bottom, to rivalry — stone competition, the exact opposite of the day's Gospel. Ride up into them with that irony in mind. Inside the walls, the Collegiata of Santa Maria Assunta holds fresco cycles of the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, and Domenico Ghirlandaio's frescoes of the town's own saint, Fina, in her chapel.7 But the church to keep for this evening is another one.

The Word

10 min
Biblical Meditation — The Prodigal Son

“There was a man who had two sons; and the younger of them said to his father, ‘Father, give me the share of property that falls to me’… the younger son gathered all he had and took his journey into a far country, and there he squandered his property in loose living… But when he came to himself he said, ‘I will arise and go to my father’… And he arose and came to his father. But while he was yet at a distance, his father saw him and had compassion, and ran and embraced him and kissed him… ‘for this my son was dead, and is alive again; he was lost, and is found.’”

Luke 15:11–24, abridged · Revised Standard Version, Catholic Edition2

Read it as a story about a road, because it is one. A young man measures out his life in advance — the share of property, the sum of everything that will one day be his — and takes it, and rides. He goes, the text says, into a far country, and he spends himself there until there is nothing left, until a famine finds him among the pigs, hungry for the husks they eat. This is what it is to travel away from mercy: not dramatic wickedness, only a slow squandering, until you look up one day and do not know how you got so far.

And then the sentence that turns the whole parable: when he came to himself. Conversion is described not as finding something new but as coming home to yourself — waking, in the pigsty, to who you actually are and whose you are. The road back is rehearsed in the mud; he practises his little speech. But he never finishes it. While he was yet at a distance, the father sees him — which means the father was watching the road — and does the undignified thing, runs, and the speech dissolves into an embrace. The son had prepared to bargain his way back to servant's wages. Mercy will not hear of it.

You are two on this road, a father and a son, and the parable will not let either of you off. To the one who has gone into the far country it says: it is never too late to come to yourself and turn. To the one who waits and watches the road it says: when the other appears, do not stand on the porch and count the cost — run. The whole of the Gospel is in that running.

Two questions for the road
  1. Where is my “far country” — the place I have quietly ridden to, a little at a time, without deciding to?
  2. Am I, today, the one coming home, or the one who must run to meet the other? What does that ask of me?

Arrival

15–20 min
Theological Reflection — Augustine's Conversion

I heard the voice as of a boy or girl… chanting, and oft repeating, “Take up and read; take up and read.”… I grasped, opened, and in silence read that paragraph on which my eyes first fell: “Not in rioting and drunkenness, not in chambering and wantonness, not in strife and envying; but put on the Lord Jesus Christ.”… No further would I read, nor did I need; for instantly… all the gloom of doubt vanished away.

Augustine, Confessions VIII.12 · trans. J. G. Pilkington4

You end the day, if you choose it well, in the right building. Inside San Gimignano's walls stands the church of Sant'Agostino, the friars' church of the Augustinian order; and across the whole apse behind its altar runs a cycle of seventeen frescoes, painted by Benozzo Gozzoli between 1463 and 1467 for the learned Augustinian friar Domenico Strambi, telling the life of Augustine himself.8 Among the scenes on that wall is one of a man in a garden, opening a book — Augustine reading the letter of Paul.8 It is the moment you have just read. You can end a day about conversion standing in front of a painting of the most famous conversion in the Western Church, in the church of his own order, at the end of a pilgrim road.

Augustine had spent years, by his own account, unable to close the gap between what he saw and what he could bring himself to do. The intellect had surrendered long before the will would follow. And when the break finally came, it came not through argument but through a child's voice over a garden wall and a single verse read almost at random. Grace did not wait for him to be worthy; it ambushed him mid-hesitation, the way the word came to Matthew at the booth and the father ran while the son was still rehearsing. This is the pattern the whole day has been drawing: mercy that does not stand on the porch.

And notice the last line: no further would I read, nor did I need. Conversion is not the end of the journey — Augustine has the whole of the Confessions, and the rest of his life, still to write. It is the point at which the doubt clears enough to keep walking. Tomorrow there is another stage, and the day after. But tonight, under towers built for rivalry, in the church of a man who learned mercy late, it is enough to have read the one verse and turned toward home.

Journal — three questions
  1. Augustine could see the good long before he could will it. Where is that gap in me, between what I know and what I do?
  2. The father ran; Christ sat at the sinners' table. Whom do I keep at arm's length that mercy would have me sit beside?
  3. “Too late did I love You.” What have I loved first, all these years, that was only ever pointing me toward the One who made it?
