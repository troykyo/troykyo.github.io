Today's Virtue — Mission

Mission
Latin missio — a sending; the pilgrimage turned outward again

Today you arrive — and arriving, you discover the road was never a dead end but a turning circle. A pilgrimage does not exist to get you to Rome. It exists to send you home changed. The last virtue is the one that faces the way you came: mission, the readiness to be sent.

Morning Reflection

The Great Commission is spoken on a mountain, and that detail matters on a morning when your own last climb is the Mount of Joy. The eleven have walked all the way to Galilee to meet the risen Christ on a hilltop — and the whole point of the summit is that they cannot stay on it. “Go,” he says. The mountaintop is not a destination but a vantage: you climb it in order to see how far the sending reaches — “all nations” — and then you come back down and start walking toward them. Every arrival in the Gospel is immediately a commissioning. There is no summit in the Christian life that is not also a trailhead.

So do not let today's joy curdle into an ending. You will roll into St Peter's Square and something in you will want to say there, done, finished, home. But you have not come to the grave of a hero; you have come to the tomb of a man who was himself sent — a fisherman told to feed sheep, a denier told three times to love, and then dispatched to the ends of the earth, ending here under this dome not as a conqueror but as a witness who was killed for the telling. Peter's tomb is not a full stop. It is an exclamation mark at the end of the word go.

And Augustine gives you the engine for it, in the five freest words he ever wrote: “Love, and do what you will.” They are not a license to do anything; they are the opposite. Get the root right — let genuine love be the thing planted deepest in you — and everything that grows up out of that root will be good, because a heart rooted in love cannot will anything else. That is the only mission worth being sent on. You did not cycle five hundred kilometres to bring Rome home as a souvenir. You came to have the root re-planted, so that whatever you now “do as you will” back in the ordinary country of your life springs from love. That is the sending. That is Day Eight.

When I am sent back down off this mountain tomorrow, into the ordinary road of my life — to whom, and to what, am I being sent?

Along the Road

10 min

The final route, Sutri to St Peter's. Map © CyclOSM / OpenStreetMap. Full elevation profile & all stages →

Through Campagnano and Formello the wooded country gradually gives way to the outskirts of the great city. Then the road joins the old line of the Via Cassia at a place with a quiet, enormous history.

La Storta — the Vision on the Road to Rome

What happened
In November 1537, travelling this very road toward Rome with his first companions Peter Faber and Diego Laynez, Ignatius of Loyola stopped to pray in a small church at La Storta — and there received the vision that would define the Jesuits: God the Father placing him with Christ, who carried the cross, and the words Ego tibi Romae propitius ero — “I will be favourable to you in Rome.”6
The uncertainty
Ignatius did not at first know what the promise meant — he thought it might mean martyrdom in Rome. It meant, instead, a mission.6
Today
The place is marked by a chapel to St Ignatius in the Piazza della Visione, the “Square of the Vision.”6
To carry
You are a pilgrim receiving, on this same road, the same word: I will be favourable to you in Rome. Not “you have arrived,” but “I go with you into what comes next.”

Monte Mario — the Mount of Joy

What
The last rise before the city, 139 metres on the right bank of the Tiber. In the Middle Ages Monte Mario lay on the Via Francigena, and the pilgrims gave it a name that says everything: Mons Gaudii — the Mount of Joy.7
Why the name
Here the long road from the north finally crested and the Leonine City — the walls of the Vatican — lay below. A million pilgrims wept and sang at this spot. You will understand why when you top it.7
To observe
Stop at the crest. Do not photograph it first. Look, and let it be joy before it is anything else.

Then the descent into the Leonine City, the colonnade opening its arms, and the dome. You have arrived. Say nothing clever. You are standing where the road has led pilgrims for more than a thousand years — over the tomb of Peter, who was sent, and could not stay.

The Word

10 min
Biblical Meditation — The Day of Pentecost

When the day of Pentecost had come, they were all together in one place. And suddenly a sound came from heaven like the rush of a mighty wind, and it filled all the house where they were sitting. And there appeared to them tongues as of fire, distributed and resting on each one of them. And they were all filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak in other tongues, as the Spirit gave them utterance.

Acts 2:1–4 · Revised Standard Version, Catholic Edition2

They had been given the Commission — “Go” — and then, tellingly, they did not go. They gathered behind closed doors and waited, because a command to be sent is not yet the power to move. For weeks the disciples were people with a mission and no engine. And then the wind, and the fire, and everything changed: the same men who had locked the doors flung them open and spilled out into the street, and the very first thing the Spirit did was untie their tongues to speak so that every foreigner in Jerusalem heard the wonders of God in his own language. Mission is not the disciples deciding to be brave. Mission is God's own breath blowing them out the door.

Watch what the fire is for. It does not rest on the group as one warm glow; it “distributes,” parting into separate flames, one on each head. Pentecost personalises. Every single one of them is set alight individually and sent individually — because the Spirit does not want a crowd, it wants a thousand particular witnesses each carrying the one fire to a place only they can reach. You are one tongue of that fire. The road you came by, the people who will be at your table next week, the corner of the world that is yours and no one else's — that is the language the Spirit means to speak through you, and no one else can speak it in your accent.

And notice where the story of Acts is going. It begins in this locked upper room in Jerusalem and it ends — the whole book ends — with the gospel reaching Rome. The city you arrive in today is, in the Bible's own geography, the far end of the sending, “the ends of the earth” toward which Pentecost aimed. You have cycled, all unknowing, to the last page of Acts. And the last page of Acts is not a conclusion; it is an open door. The Spirit that filled that room is the same Spirit ready to fill you at journey's end — not to reward the arrival, but to fuel the going home.

Two questions for the road
  1. The disciples had the mission but waited for the power. Where am I trying to be sent on my own strength, with the doors still shut?
  2. The fire “distributed” — one flame each. What is my tongue of it: the language, the place, the people only I can reach?

Arrival

15–20 min
Theological Reflection — Leo the Great on the True Greatness of Rome

…the head of the world through blessed Peter's holy See, you attained a wider sway by the worship of God than by earthly government.

Pope St Leo the Great, Sermon 82.1, on the Feast of the Apostles Peter and Paul · trans. C. L. Feltoe4

You have arrived in the city that once believed itself eternal — and it is right that a Roman pope should have the last word to you here, because Leo the Great, preaching in this city in the fifth century, understood exactly what a pilgrim needs to understand on the day of arrival. Rome, he tells his people, was great once: mistress of nations, ruler of an empire, its eagles over half the world. But all of that, he says, was the lesser greatness. The city became truly great only when two travellers were killed in it for love of Christ — Peter and Paul, whose tombs you now stand over — and through their witness Rome “attained a wider sway by the worship of God than by earthly government.” The empire conquered bodies. The apostles conquered hearts, and the empire is dust while the sending goes on.

Sit with that, because it reframes everything you have done. You did not ride here to touch the greatness of an empire. You rode to the graves of two men whose entire importance is that they were sent and went. Peter came to Rome to die telling the truth; Paul came in chains and kept preaching from them. Neither of them arrived in the sense your legs are begging for tonight — a rest, a full stop, a task completed. They arrived the way a seed arrives in the ground: as the beginning of something that outlives the arriver. The greatness of this place is not that the road ends here. It is that from here the road ran out to the whole world — and is still running, through you, tonight.

So this is the last reflection, and it will not let you keep the pilgrimage as a memory to put on a shelf. Rome is not the conclusion of your journey. Rome is the hinge. Eight days ago the road called you out of your ordinary life; tonight, at the tomb of the Apostles, it turns you around and sends you back into it — not the same person who set out, but one who has learned obedience on the first day and mission on the last, and hope and humility and gratitude in between. Leo would tell you plainly: the worship of God has a “wider sway” than any government, and it advances not by armies but by ordinary people who were sent home changed. Go, then. You are one of them now.

Journal — three questions for the last night
  1. Leo says Rome's true greatness is not its empire but its witnesses. Looking back over eight days, what in me was “empire” — and what became witness?
  2. Name the eight virtues you carried, one per day — obedience, mercy, charity, humility, gratitude, hope, longing, mission. Which one is God asking to be the root of the year ahead?
  3. “I will be favourable to you in Rome.” The promise was never about staying. To what one person or task am I being sent home, and what is the first thing I will do?
