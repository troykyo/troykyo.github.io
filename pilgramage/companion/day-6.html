Today's Virtue — Hope

Hope
Latin spes — the settled expectation of a promised good

You cross a border today. New region, new river, new lake — and, if you let it, a renewed heart. Hope is not optimism about the weather; it is the theological certainty that the good you are riding toward has been promised, and that the One who promised it is faithful.

Scripture

Every one who drinks of this water will thirst again, but whoever drinks of the water that I shall give him will never thirst; the water that I shall give him will become in him a spring of water welling up to eternal life.

John 4:13–14 · Revised Standard Version, Catholic Edition1

From Augustine

“Hope has for its object only what is good, only what is future, and only what affects the man who entertains the hope.” Augustine, Enchiridion (Handbook on Faith, Hope, and Charity), ch. 83

Morning Reflection

Augustine draws a fine, freeing line. Faith, he says, can be about anything — the past, the present, the future, things that happened to others, matters that touch you not at all. But hope has three strict conditions, and they are worth carrying today. Hope is only ever about what is good: you cannot hope for an evil, only dread it. Hope is only ever about what is future: you cannot hope for what you already hold. And hope is only ever about what concerns you: it is not a spectator's virtue but a traveller's. Which is to say — hope is exactly the shape of a pilgrimage. A good, still out ahead, that is genuinely yours to reach.

You leave Tuscany today. Behind you on its black rock stands Radicofani, remembered for an outlaw — a monument to a past that cannot be changed. Ahead of you lies water: the Paglia to ford, and then the enormous calm eye of Lake Bolsena, filling an old volcano to the brim. It is the right image to ride toward. Yesterday was about memory, which looks back; today is about hope, which looks forward — and the hinge between them is water that never runs dry. “A spring welling up to eternal life,” Jesus tells the woman who came to a well with an empty jar and a thirst she thought was only physical.

That is the difference between a cistern and a spring, and it is worth knowing which one your hope is built on. A cistern holds what you have poured in; when the summer is long, it fails, and you find yourself scraping the bottom of your own resources. A spring is fed from somewhere else, underground, out of your control and beyond your management, and it does not depend on you at all. Christian hope is spring-fed. It does not run on how the legs feel at kilometre forty, or on the mood at the top of the climb. It runs on a promise made by Someone else. Ride toward the water.

Is my hope today a cistern — drawing anxiously on my own reserves — or a spring, fed by a promise I did not make and cannot exhaust?

Along the Road

10 min

Today's route, Radicofani to Bolsena. Map © CyclOSM / OpenStreetMap. Full elevation profile & all stages →

Off the descent you cross the Paglia and, with it, the old frontier: Tuscany behind, Lazio — the country of Rome — ahead. The first town on the far side is Acquapendente, whose cathedral dates to 1149 and which the pilgrim tradition marks as the first Italian stop in the travels of St Roch, patron of pilgrims and the plague-stricken.7 Then the planned papal town of San Lorenzo Nuovo, and the road tips downhill to the lake.

Bolsena — the Basilica of Santa Cristina & the Eucharistic Miracle

Photo: Sidvics (CC BY-SA 3.0) · Wikimedia Commons
The miracle
In 1263, in this basilica, a Bohemian priest in doubt about the doctrine of Transubstantiation reported that the host he had consecrated at Mass began to bleed.6
The corporal
The blood-stained corporal (the linen cloth of the altar) was carried to nearby Orvieto, whose great cathedral was built to house it — the relic set in a reliquary made by the Sienese goldsmith Ugolino di Vieri in 1337–1338.6
Why it matters
Bolsena is called the city of the Eucharistic miracle, the place from which the solemnity of Corpus Domini — Corpus Christi — spread through the whole Catholic Church.6
The saint
The basilica bears the name of St Christina, martyr and patron of Bolsena — whose feast is today, 24 July. You arrive on her day.6
To observe
The lake itself: a volcanic crater filled to its rim, the largest of its kind in Europe. Stand at the water's edge and read again the morning's Gospel about the spring that never fails.
Practical
Basilica opening hours and Mass times vary by season — confirm on site or at the church door. Route information: viefrancigene.org.

The Word

10 min
Biblical Meditation — Walking on the Water

And in the fourth watch of the night he came to them, walking on the sea… And Peter answered him, “Lord, if it is you, bid me come to you on the water.” He said, “Come.” So Peter got out of the boat and walked on the water and came to Jesus; but when he saw the wind, he was afraid, and beginning to sink he cried out, “Lord, save me.” Jesus immediately reached out his hand and caught him, saying to him, “O man of little faith, why did you doubt?”

Matthew 14:25, 28–31 · Revised Standard Version, Catholic Edition2

It is a story about a lake, and today you finish beside one, so let it be your meditation. The disciples are straining at the oars in the dark, the wind against them, and then the very thing that should terrify them — a figure walking on the water — turns out to be their rescue. Hope often comes disguised as the thing we fear. And Peter, gloriously, impulsively, does the most hopeful thing in all the Gospels: he asks to be let out of the boat.

Notice what actually holds him up. It is not his courage, which is real but brief. It is not his skill, for there is no technique for walking on water. It is not even his faith, considered as a quantity he possesses — because his faith fails him halfway across, and down he goes. What holds Peter up is a Person, and a hand. He walks while he looks at Christ; he sinks the instant he looks at the wind. The waves did not change. The wind did not get worse. He simply transferred his attention from the One who called him to the trouble all around him — and that shift of the eyes was the whole of his sinking.

There is a priest in this town's history who doubted — who could not believe that Christ was truly present under the appearance of ordinary bread — and whose doubt, the tradition says, was answered not with an argument but with blood. Peter, sinking, does the only thing left to a drowning man: three words, “Lord, save me,” and instantly — Matthew says immediately — the hand is there. Hope is not the absence of the sinking feeling. Hope is knowing whose hand catches you when you sink.

Two questions for the road
  1. What is the “wind” — the fear, the circumstance — that pulls my eyes off Christ and starts me sinking?
  2. Peter's prayer was three words. When did I last simply cry, “Lord, save me,” instead of trying to swim?

Arrival

15–20 min
Theological Reflection — Benedict on Running the Way

But as we advance in the religious life and faith, we shall run the way of God's commandments with expanded hearts and unspeakable sweetness of love; so that never departing from His guidance and persevering in the monastery in His doctrine till death, we may by patience share in the sufferings of Christ, and be found worthy to be coheirs with Him of His kingdom.

St Benedict, Rule, Prologue · trans. Boniface Verheyen, OSB4

Six days in, your body understands this sentence better than it did on Day 1. Benedict is describing exactly what you are feeling. At the start, the way of God's commandments is all narrowness and effort — the road is “strait,” he says a few lines earlier, and beginnings always are. But he promises that something changes as you go: the heart dilates, widens, the labour becomes gladness, and you find yourself no longer trudging the commandments but running them, “with unspeakable sweetness of love.” That is the pilgrim's hope in a single image. The road does not get shorter. You get larger.

And notice how honest he is about the mechanism: this hope runs on perseverance. Elsewhere in the Rule, among his tools of the spiritual craft, Benedict lists one that belongs to this exact day: “never to despair of God's mercy.”4 He puts it last, deliberately, as the tool you reach for when all the others have failed — the final refusal to give up on grace. This is why the monk is the great teacher of hope: not because he expects an easy road, but because he has taken a vow of stability, a promise to persevere in one place, through the dry seasons, until the spring is found again. Peter sank because he stopped; the monk runs because he refuses to.

You slept last night on a fortress and you sleep tonight beside a lake, in a town whose whole meaning is that Christ is really, bodily, hopefully present — even, especially, to the one who doubts. Tomorrow the road turns decisively toward Rome, and something in you will begin to lean forward, to smell the end of the journey. That leaning is hope, and Benedict blesses it: run the way with an expanded heart. Only — do not run to escape the road. Run because the sweetness has finally caught up with the effort.

Journal — three questions
  1. Benedict says the heart dilates as we go. Where, over these six days, has something that was pure effort at the start quietly become gladness?
  2. His last tool is “never to despair of God's mercy.” In what corner of my life have I quietly given up on grace — decided it will not reach there?
  3. Tomorrow the road turns toward Rome. What am I now genuinely hoping for at the end — and is it good, future, and truly mine?
